1. What was the routine of Mr. Chips after rejoining Brookfield?

About half past ten every morning, Mr. Chips put on his coat and muffler to go to the school. He took just a few forms in Latin and Roman history. He taught the same old lessons with old pronounciation.

2. How did Chips feel after rejoining Brookfield?

After rejoining Brookfield, Chips felt like a music hall favourite returning to the boards after a positive last appearance. Physically, he felt himself quite fit. For the first time in his life, he felt himself necessary to something that was nearest to his heart.

3. What kind of jokes did Chips make when he joined Brookfield again during the war?

Chips made new jokes about the O.T.C. and the food rationing system and the anti-airraid blinds that had to be fitted on all the windows. There was a mysterious kind of dish that was served on Mondays, and Chips called it "Abhorrendum" ‐ "meat to be abhorred".

4. Why was Chips made the Acting Head of Brookfield for the second time?

Chips became the Acting Head of Brookfield for the second time in 1917. He was the acting head because Chatteris fell ill.

5. When did Chatteris die?

Chatteris was diabetic. He fell ill during the winter term and he died in April 1917.

6. Why Mr. Chips did not want to be appointed the permanent head of Brookfield?

Mr. Chips did not want to be appointed the permanent head of Brookfield because he felt himself unequal to that job in so many ways. He did not want the people to expect a lot from him in his old age.

7. What kind of work did Chips do when he became the acting head of Brookfield for the second time?

As the head of Brookfield, Mr. Chips sat in the headmaster's study every morning, handled problems, dealt with complaints and requests. On Sundays, in chapel, it was he who now read out the tragic list.

8. How did Chips perform his duties during his second headship?

During his second headship, Mr. Chips did his job quite efficiently. He used his vast experience with kindly gentle confidence to promote sense of proportion in Brookfield. He handled problems, dealt with complaints and requests quite confidently as well.

9. How did Chips feel when he read out the tragic list of the people killed in the war?

The names read out from the tragic list on Sundays during the chapel service were not mere names for Chips. He could still see their faces. That's why, sometimes he was heard and seen in tears over it.

10. What did Mr. Chips feel after announcing the name of Max Stefel, the German master, among the Brookfieldians killed in the war?

At first, Mr. Chips felt rather pale because announcing the death of a German among the English was altogether unusual. However, he was not displeased at all by his comment at all. He considered his comment as generosity and dignity.

11. What did people say when Chips read out the name of Max Stefel, the German master, among the Brookfieldians killed in the war?

When Chips read out the name of Max Staefel with all the others, people thought it was funny to include him on the tragic list because, after all, he was an enemy. They, however, ascribed it to Chips' old ideas.

12. What did Chips say when he was asked for his opinion of bayonet practice?

When Chips was asked for his opinion of bayonet practice, he said that it was a very vulgar way of killing people. From this incident, people found an adjective for him; they began to say Chips was 'pre-war'.

13. What is Rissole? What joke did Chips make about it?

Rissole is a small croquette, enclosed in pastry or rolled in breadcrumbs, most often filled with minced meat or fish, which is usually baked or deep fried. When it was added to the school menu on Mondays, Chips called it 'Abhorrendum' ‐ "meat to be abhorred". This became the latest joke of Mr. Chips.
