1. Where did Mr. Chips live after his retirement from Brookfield? OR How long did Mr. Chips stay at Mrs. Wickett's?

Mr. Chips lived at Mrs. Wickett's after his retirement from Brookfield. Mrs. Wickett's house was just across the road from Brookfield. He had been living there for more than a decade (10 years).

2. How did Mr. Chips measure his time when he lived at Mrs. Wickett's house?

At Mrs. Wickett's, Mr. Chips, like some old sea-captain, measured his time by the signals of the past. The proceedings of Brookfield were the signals of past for both Chips and Mrs. Wickett.

3. How did Mr. Chips pass his retired life?

Mr. Chips lived a cozy and peaceful life at Mrs. Wickett's after his retirement. He sat by the fire and drank tea and listened to school bells.

4. How did Chips end his daily activities at Mrs. Wickett's?

At the end of his day, Mr. Chips would wind up the clock after the last bell at Brookfield. Then he would turn out the gas and carry a detective novel to the bed to enjoy a peaceful sleep.

5. Who was Merivale? OR What did Mr. Merivale say about Mr. Chips' health?

Mr. Merivale was the physician of Chips. He used to visit Mr. Chips every fortnight or so. According to him, there was really nothing the matter with Mr. Chips. He said that Mr. Chips was going to die a normal death as he was past the age when people got horrible diseases.

6. What advice did Merivale give to Mrs. Wickett about Chips?

Dr. Merivale would sometimes advise her to look after Mr. Chips as his chest put strain on his heart. He usually advised so when Chips had a cold or when east winds roared over the fenlands.

7. When did Mr. Chips join Brookfield? What was it like?

Mr. Chips joined Brookfield in 1870. That year was easy to remember for him because of the Franco-Prussian War. He had been at Melbury for a year before he joined Brookfield.

It was a beautiful sunny day when Mr. Chips came to Brookfield for his preliminary interview. He met Wetherby during this interview for the first time. Mr. Wetherby gave him many valuable pieces of advice.

8. Why did Mr. Chips did not like Melbury?

Mr. Chips disliked Melbury because he had been ragged there a good deal. Actually, discipline was not always his strong point at Melbury.

9. What kind of person was Mr. Wetherby?

Mr. Wetherby was the headmaster of Brookfield. He was a very fatherly and courteous person. He helped Brookfield a lot to be a front rank school.

10. What did Mr. Wetherby advise Mr. Chips?

Mr. Wetherby advised Mr. Chips regarding his personal and disciplinary improvement. He advised Mr. Chips to give his enthusiasm to Brookfield in order to gain something in return. He told him not to let anyone play tricks with him, and to take up a firm attitude from the beginning.

11. When did Wetherby die?

Wetherby died in 1870, during the summer vacation. He died even before Chips had begun his first term at Brookfield.

12. How did Mr. Chips face his first class at Brookfield?

Mr. Chips looked quite young and fresh complexioned. He wore high collars and had side whiskers at that time.

His first class was a prep class. The Big Hall was full of five hundred unprincipled and barbaric students. Initially, he found himself at the mercy of these ruffians. However, he took the firm attitude and punished the boy who had tried to create disturbance.

13. Describe the students in the first class of Mr. Chips.

The students in the first class of Mr. Chips were like lusty barbarians and unprincipled ruffians. They thought of Mr. Chips as their legitimate prey. Teasing new teachers was a fine art, an exciting sport and something of a tradition to them.

14. Who was Colley and why did Chips punish him?

Colley was the first student at Brookfield whom Mr. Chips punished during his first class. He tried to create disturbance in the class by dropping the desk lid. Mr. Chips punished him by giving him a hundred lines.

15. How did time seem to pass for Chips when he was living a retired life?

Mr. Chips was getting on in year. He got sleepy at times and time seemed to pass like lazy cattle moving across a landscape, when he was living his retired life.

16. How was it possible for Chips to live according to the timetable of Brookfield after retirement?

Mr. Chips had a touchy relation with the students and he felt very comfortable with them. After retirement, he was living just across the road from the school. Therefore, it was quite easy for him to live according to Brookfield's timetable as he could hear the bells.

17. How much did Chips read before sleeping?

Before sleeping, he used to read a detective novel. He read only a page of it then sleep came to him swiftly and peacefully.

18. When was Chips born? What was his age when he saw the Great Exhibition?

Chips was born in 1848. He was taken as a toddling child to the Great Exhibition (also known as the Crystal Palace Exhibition) in London, when he was only three years old.

19. What was the duration of Mr. Wetherby as the headmaster of Brookfield?

Mr. Wetherby was the headmaster of Brookfield from 1840 to 1870. During his tenure, he made valuable contributions to Brookfield.

20. Describe the day when Chips came to Brookfield for interview.

It was a sunny July, with air full of flower scents. A match of cricket was being played between Brookfield and Barnhurst. A chubby little fellow, who was one of the Barnhurst boys, made a brilliant century against Brookfield.

21. Describe the atmosphere of Chips' first class.

There was a sudden hush when he took his place at the desk on the dais. A tall clock was ticking behind him. There was smell of ink and varnish. Blood-red rays were slanting in slabs through the stained-glass windows.

22. What did Chips say to young Colley, the son of the Colley whom Chips had punished in his first class at Brookfield?

Chips said to young Colley, "Colley, your father was the first boy I ever punished when I came here twenty-five years ago. He deserved it then, and you deserve it now."

23. What did Chips say to the grandson of the first Colley?

Chips said to the grandson of the first Colley, punctuating his remarks with a little "umph-um" that he had by then, "Colley, you are a splendid example of inherited traditions. I remember your grandfather, he could never grasp the Ablative Absolute. A stupid fellow, your grandfather. And your father, too. I remember him, he used to sit at that far desk by the wall. He wasn't much better, either. But I do believe, my dear Colley, that you are the biggest fool of the lot!".
