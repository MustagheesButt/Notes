19.1 What are the measurements on which two observers in relative motion will always agree upon?

Two observers in relative motion will always agree upon the following measurements:
  • Force
  • Speed of light

19.2 Does the dilation means that time really passes more slowly in moving system or that it only seems to pass more slowly?

Time actually passes more slowly in the moving reference frame. It is not just that it seems this way, it has actually been measured to pass more slowly, as predicted by special relativity. It happens when observers are in relative motion. The result is applied to the timing processes physical, chemical and biological. Even aging process of the human body is slowed down by motion at a very high speed.

This phenomenon cannot be observed in daily life because we don't move at a speed comparable to the speed of light.

19.3 If you are moving in a spaceship at a very high speed relative to the Earth, would you notice a difference
  1. in your pulse rate
  2. in the pulse rate of people on Earth?

  1. No. There will be no difference in the pulse rate as the inertial frame of reference is same.
  2. Yes. The pulse rate of the people on Earth will be low because they are moving relative to the spaceship.

19.4 If the speed of light were infinite, what would the equations of special theory of relativity reduce to?

If speed of light (c) approaches infinity, then energy (E) becomes infinity according to the formula
  • E = mc2
  • E = m(∞)2
  • E = ∞
Equations of special theory of relativity would become,
  1. \( t = \frac{ t_{o} }{ \sqrt{ 1 - \frac{ v^{2} }{ c^{2} } } } = \frac{ t_{o} }{ \sqrt{ 1 - \frac{ v^{2} }{ \infty^{2} } } } = t_{o} \)
  2. \( l = \frac{ l_{o} }{ \sqrt{ 1 - \frac{ v^{2} }{ c^{2} } } } = \frac{ l_{o} }{ \sqrt{ 1 - \frac{ v^{2} }{ \infty^{2} } } } = l_{o} \)
  3. \( m = \frac{ m_{o} }{ \sqrt{ 1 - \frac{ v^{2} }{ c^{2} } } } = \frac{ m_{o} }{ \sqrt{ 1 - \frac{ v^{2} }{ \infty^{2} } } } = m_{o} \)

19.5 Since mass is a form of energy, can we conclude that a compressed spring has more mass than the same spring when it is not compressed?

From classical point of view, the mass remains same. According to the theory of relativity (E = mc2), mass increases because the compressed spring has more potential energy which was given to it by some external source (for compressing). However, this increase in mass is so small that it can be ignored.

19.6 As a solid is heated and begins to glow, why does it first appear red?

According to Wein's displacement law, a solid first glows in red colour because at low temperature body emits radiations having long wavelengths. On further heating, it emits radiations of shorter wavelengths.

19.7 What happens to total radiation from a blackbody if its absolute temperature is doubled?

According to Boltzmann law, if the absolute temperature is doubled, then total radiation from a blackbody becomes 16 times greater:
  • E = σT4
  • when T = 2T,
  • E' = 16(σT4)
  • E' = 16E

19.8 A beam of red light and a beam of blue light have exactly the same energy. Which beam contains the greater number of photons?

The energy of the photon , according to Planck's Quantum theory is given by E = hf, where h is the Planck's constant and f is the frequency of light.

Let n1 be the number of photons of red light having frequency f1 and n2 be the number of photons of blue light having frequency f2. The respective energies of red and blue light are given by:

  • E1 = n1hf1
  • E2 = n2hf2
  • Both have same level of energy, E1 = E2
  • n1hf1 = n2hf2
  • f1/f2 = n1/n2
  • As, f1 < f2
  • Hence, n2 < n1
Therefore, the number of photons of red light (n1) are more than the number of photons of blue light (n2).

19.9 Which photon, red, green, or blue carries the most
  1. energy and
  2. momentum?

As E = hf, p = hf/c and f = c/λ. Therefore,
  • E = hc/λ
  • p = h/λ
As blue light has smaller wavelength than that of green and red, so photons of blue light carry the most energy and momentum.

19.10 Which has the lower energy quanta? Radiowaves or X-rays.

According to Planck's Quantum theory, E = hf. As X-rays have higher frequency than radiowaves, so radiowaves have lower energy quanta than X-rays.

19.11 Does the brightness of a beam of light primarily depends on the frequency of photons or on the number of photons?

Brightness or the Intensity of a light beam means the number of photons of light passing per second per unit area (NA-1s-1). So, the brightness depends upon the number of photons and not on the frequency of photons.

19.12 When ultraviolet light falls on certain dyes, visible light is emitted. Why does this not happen when infrared light falls on these dyes?

The ultraviolet light contains high energy photons. When they fall on the atoms of the dye, the atoms get excited. On de-excitation, they emit photons of frequency which lie in the visible region of the electromagnetic spectrum. However, infrared light photons have less energy, so the atoms may get excited, on de-excitation they emit photons whose frequency lie in the invisible region of the electromagnetic spectrum.

19.13 Will bright light eject more electrons from a metal surface than dimmer light of the same colour?

The number of photoelectrons emitted from the metal surface is proportional to the intensity of light. So bright light will eject more electrons from the metal surface than the dimmer light of the same color (frequency).

19.14 Will higher frequency light eject greater number of electrons than low frequency light?

The number of photoelectrons emitted from the metal surface is directly proportional to the intensity of the incident light and not on its frequency. The higher frequency light will eject high-energy electrons, while lower frequency light will eject low-energy electrons. Therefore, light of higher and lower frequency of same intensity, both will eject same number of electrons.

19.15 When light shines on a surface, is momentum transferred to the metal surface?

When light falls on metal surface, the photons are absorbed by the surface. Therefore, both energy and momentum of the photons are transferred to the atoms of the surface. This transfer of energy and momentum to the surface is so small that it produces nearly no disturbance in the photo-emissive surface.

19.16 Why can red light be used in a photographic dark room when developing films, but a blue or white light cannot?

Photons of red light have the longest wavelength and the lowest energy. Therefore, photographic films are not affected. The use of blue of white light may damage the photographic films.

19.18 Why don't we observe a Compton effect with visible light?

For Compton effect, we need energy between 0.5 MeV to 1.02 MeV. The energy of visible light is 3.1 eV, which is much less than 0.5 MeV, therefore, the energy of visible light is not enough for Compton effect to take place.

19.19 Can pair production take place in vacuum? Explain.

No. Pair production occurs when photons with sufficient energy interact with the nucleus to produce mass. Laws of conservation of momentum and conservation of energy will be violated if pair production takes place in vacuum. Therefore, pair production cannot take place in vacuum as it does not contain any matter.

19.20 Is it possible to create a single electron from energy? Explain.

No. Due to the law of conservation of charge, the creation of an electron is always accompanied by a positron. Therefore, it is not possible to create a single electron from energy alone.

19.21 If electrons behaved only like particles, what pattern would you expect on the screen after the electrons have passed through the double slit?

If electrons behaved like particles, diffraction will not take place and electrons would pass straight through the slits. Therefore, the diffraction pattern on the screen will not be observed. Only the images of the two slits will be observed on the screen.

19.22 If an electron and a proton have the same de Broglie wavelength, which particle has greater speed?

As,
  • Electron's de Broglie wavelength = Proton's de Broglie wavelength
  • h/meve = h/mpvp
  • mpvp = meve
  • As, mp > me
  • So, ve > vp
Therefore, the electron has greater speed.

19.23 We do not notice the de Broglie wavelength for a pitched cricket ball. Explain why?

  • According to de Broglie, wavelength of cricket ball λ = h/mv
  • As, h = 6.63×10-34
  • λ = 6.63×10-34/mv
As the cricket ball is heavy, the de Broglie wavelength is so small that it is not noticeable with the naked eye.

19.24 If the following particles have the same energy, which has the shortest wavelength? Electron, alpha particle, neutron, proton.

According to de Broglie equation, λ = h/mv. For same energy (or K.E.) v and h are constant, so

λ ∝ 1/m

It follows from the above equation that for greater mass the wavelength is shorter. As α-particle has the greatest mass, so it has the shortest wavelength, and that the electron has the longest wavelength because it has the smallest mass.

19.25 When does light behave as a wave? When does it behave as a particle?

Light behaves as wave in case of Interference, Diffraction, and Refraction.

Light behaves as particle in case of Pair Production, Compton Effect, and Photoelectric Effect.

19.26 What advantages an electron microscope has over an optical microscope?

The electron microscope has the following advantages over optical microscope:
  • The wavelength associated with electrons is much shorter than the visible light, so electron microscope has a high resolving power as compared to optical microscope.
  • The energy of electrons is large enough to penetrate through thick parts of matter. Therefore, the internal structure of an object can be obtained by using an electron microscope.

19.27 If measurements show a precise position for an electron, can those measurements show precise momentum also? Explain.

No. If measurements show a precise position of an electron then precise momentum can't be measured because the Uncertainty principle states that both position and momentum of a particle can't be measured simultaneously with perfect accuracy. This is known as Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle.
